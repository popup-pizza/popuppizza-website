Dough Calculator

Free Tool

This is the exact calculator we use to make our own pizza dough, exact ingredient weights for any batch size, and the right water temperature for perfect dough every time. Everything is measured in baker's percentages: every ingredient is expressed as a percentage of the flour weight, which is always treated as 100%. It's how professional bakers scale recipes up or down without redoing the maths each time, and once you're used to it, it's genuinely simpler than cups and spoons.

New to this? Start with a style preset below (try Neapolitan or New York), then just change "Number of pizzas" to however many you're making. Everything else recalculates automatically. If you want to understand what each number actually does, keep an eye on @pop_uppizza, we'll be breaking down the details there.

Ingredient Ratios

%

This is the single biggest lever for how your dough feels and bakes. Lower (55–62%) gives a firmer, easier-to-handle dough and a crispier base, good for beginners and thin styles. Higher (70%+) gives an airier, more open crumb but the dough is wetter and stickier to work with, that's the trade-off Neapolitan and Roman pizzerias accept for the texture.

Oil excluded

Salt controls flavour and also slows fermentation, too little and the dough over-proves quickly; too much and it can slow yeast activity right down. Yeast is what makes the dough rise, more yeast means a faster rise but less developed flavour, less yeast means a slower, longer ferment with more flavour (this is why Neapolitan uses so little). Oil is optional and mostly affects texture, it makes the crumb softer and helps browning, which is why Neapolitan traditionally skips it for a crisper, more blistered result.

Water Temperature (Desired Dough Temp)

Why this matters: yeast is sensitive to temperature, and the temperature your dough ends up at right after mixing has a big effect on how it ferments from that point on. The only variable you can easily control on the day is the temperature of the water you mix with, everything else (room, flour, your mixer's own heat) is fixed. This calculator works backwards from your target dough temperature to tell you exactly what water temperature to use.

Target dough temp is the temperature you want the dough to be immediately after mixing, before it goes anywhere, usually 22–26°C. This is separate from where the dough ferments afterwards: our own house recipe is mixed to around 24°C, then moved into the fridge to cold-ferment at 8°C for 72 hours. A long, cold ferment like this develops flavour slowly without needing much yeast at all, which is why our house recipe uses just 0.05% fresh yeast. Friction factor is the heat your mixer adds while kneading, hand-mixing barely warms the dough, while a stand mixer running for several minutes adds noticeably more heat. If you're not sure what to use, the friction factor helper below will estimate it from a batch you've already made.

What counts as "room temperature"? This varies a lot more than people expect, a kitchen in summer can easily be 28°C–30°C, while the same room in winter might sit at 16°C–18°C. Since this number directly changes your water temperature result, it's worth actually measuring your kitchen rather than guessing. A few reasonable starting points if you can't measure it right now:

Air-conditioned kitchen, most of the year20–22°C
Unconditioned kitchen, mild weather18–24°C
Unconditioned kitchen, Australian summer26–30°C+
Unconditioned kitchen, Australian winter14–18°C

Friction Factor Helper, work out yours from a batch you've made

Friction factor is the heat your mixer (or your hands) add to the dough while kneading. It's specific to your equipment, so it's more accurate to measure it once than to guess. These are typical starting ranges:

Spiral mixer12–18°C
Stand mixer20–26°C
Hand-mixing6–10°C

To measure yours: next time you make a batch, take the dough's temperature right after mixing, before it starts to rise. Enter your numbers below and this will work out your actual friction factor. If you can, do this for two or three batches and average the results, a thermometer reading taken even a minute or two late will throw the number off.
